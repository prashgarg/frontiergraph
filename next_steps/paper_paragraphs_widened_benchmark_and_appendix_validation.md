# Paper Paragraphs: Widened Benchmark and Appendix Validation

## Methods paragraph: widened historical benchmark

Our main historical benchmark uses the current effective-corpus graph and evaluates five-year cutoff snapshots from 1990 through 2015, with horizon-valid subsets for `h=5`, `h=10`, and `h=15`. At each cutoff `t`, the graph uses the full literature through `t-1`; older support is downweighted by recency decay rather than removed by a hard rolling window. Widening the benchmark confirms the main ranking result, but it also shows that early years are not just thinner versions of the later sample. Relative to post-2000 cutoffs, the 1990 and 1995 cutoffs have fewer realized positives and surface candidate objects with younger support, higher recent-share measures, and lower diversity and stability. We therefore treat early years as a distinct historical regime rather than as simple noise.

## Appendix paragraph: LLM usefulness sweep

As a supplementary current-usefulness check, we run a blinded LLM evaluation on the widened historical shortlist objects. The model sees only a raw triplet `A -> B -> C` and a short construction note explaining that the middle node is an intervening concept from the literature graph; it may represent a mechanism, channel, condition, policy lever, or other bridge. It is asked to rate four narrow dimensions: readability, meaning whether the displayed labels are easy to read and not obviously malformed; interpretability, meaning whether the reader can tell what relationship or bridge is being proposed; usefulness, meaning whether the object looks like a usable research question even if it still needs revision; and artifact risk, meaning whether it reads like a graph artifact rather than a genuine question object. The prompt explicitly excludes novelty at the cutoff, likely future success, and topic importance. On the full widened grid (`17` exercises, top `250`, and three arms: adopted, transparent, and preferential attachment), the pooled ordering is adopted > transparent > preferential attachment, but this comparison is era-sensitive: in the early era transparent objects can look slightly cleaner than adopted ones, whereas in the later era adopted clearly looks better on the same current-usefulness rubric. We therefore use this exercise only as appendix robustness evidence, not as the main historical benchmark.

## Validation paragraph: human usefulness pack

To provide an external non-LLM usefulness check on the same object, we compare a small blinded set of items chosen by the graph method with a matched set chosen by a preferential-attachment baseline. Each item is shown as a raw triplet plus the same construction note used in the appendix LLM pass, and raters score the same four dimensions: readability, interpretability, usefulness, and artifact risk. Here readability is a narrow surface measure, mainly whether the labels are clean and easy to read, while interpretability and usefulness are the more substantive dimensions. In the current 24-item pack, the result is mixed rather than one-sided. Graph-selected items are somewhat more interpretable, slightly more useful, and slightly less artifact-like, but the preferential-attachment items read a little more smoothly on average. There is no overall mean-score advantage for the graph-selected set. We therefore use the human exercise as cautious external validation, not as evidence of a large human-perceived quality gap.
