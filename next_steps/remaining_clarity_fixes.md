# Remaining Clarity Fixes (Comprehensive)

After the 18-fix pass, the main structural issues are resolved. What remains is a final polish layer: density in specific paragraphs, a few stale phrases, and figure-note consistency. These are all small but they add up.

---

## A. Inline number density (report fewer numbers, use plain language)

### 1. Section 5.2 (attention frontier) — still number-heavy
Line ~636: "At h=3, preferential attachment places about 11.0 future links per 100 suggestions at K=100, compared with 5.75 for the graph score. By K=1000, the two rules are essentially tied... 4.25 versus 4.70. The same pattern appears at h=5: the gap is 14.75 versus 8.25 at K=100, but 6.83 versus 7.10 by K=1000. At h=10..."

This is 12 numbers in one paragraph. A labor economist will zone out.

**Fix:** Report one horizon (h=5) inline with two numbers, then say "the same pattern holds at all horizons" with a pointer to the figure.

### 2. Section 5.4 (path development) — shares reported to 3 decimal places
Line ~719: "the direct-to-path share rises from roughly 0.049 in the 1980s to 0.089 in the 1990s, 0.178 in the 2000s, and 0.355 in the 2010s. The corresponding path-to-direct shares are much smaller: about 0.023, 0.014, 0.015, and 0.020."

Eight numbers, all transition shares that mean nothing to a reader without context.

**Fix:** Replace with: "Direct-to-path transitions dominate path-to-direct transitions by a ratio of roughly 3:1 in the 1990s, rising to about 18:1 in the 2010s." That communicates the magnitude without numbers that require mental division.

### 3. Section 5.2 (attention frontier) — h=10 sub-paragraph
"the tight-budget gap remains larger, yet even there the frontier narrows substantially, from 27.5 versus 17.75 at K=100 to 13.6 versus 13.5 by K=1000"

**Fix:** "even at h=10, the gap narrows sharply: from a 55-percent advantage for preferential attachment at K=100 to near parity by K=1000."

## D. Stale or unclear phrases

### 9. "measurement anchor" vs "benchmark anchor" vs "retrieval anchor"
The paper uses three different terms for the same object. Line 68 (abstract): "missing directed links." Line 81: "benchmarkable retrieval anchors." Line 83: "measurement anchor."

**Fix:** Pick one and use it everywhere. "Benchmark anchor" is the clearest. Or just say "missing link" consistently.

### 10. "surfaced object" / "surfaced question"
Used interchangeably. "Surfaced" is not standard economics vocabulary — it sounds like project jargon. An economist would say "the question the system presents to the reader" or just "the question."

**Fix:** Replace "surfaced object" with "the question the reader inspects" or "the candidate question" throughout the main text. Keep "surfaced" only where it's a section title or term of art defined earlier.

### 11. "the paper's honest benchmark claim" (not in the current text, but watch for)
Self-praising language. Economics papers should state claims, not describe them as honest.

### 12. "the right conclusion" (line ~592)
"The right conclusion from the strict headline is not 'the graph score fails.'" — Telling the reader what the right conclusion is sounds defensive.
**Fix:** "The strict headline does not mean the graph score fails. It means..."

## E. Table formatting

### 13. Notation table (line ~262)
The notation table defines G_{t-1}, u, v, h, K. It's useful but comes at the end of Section 3, after the reader has already seen most of these symbols. It should come earlier — ideally right after the pipeline overview figure.

### 14. Appendix schema tables (lines ~808, ~832)
The node-schema and edge-schema tables are useful for replication but very long (35+ rows combined). Consider moving them to a separate online appendix or reducing to the 10 most important fields.
